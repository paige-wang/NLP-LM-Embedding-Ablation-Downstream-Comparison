% ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This report presents a three-part empirical study on language modeling, embedding initialization, and representation transfer.
In the first part, four language model families---trigram, RNN, LSTM, and a custom decoder-only Transformer---are trained on WikiText-2 and compared in perplexity, training efficiency, and generation quality.
The Transformer achieves the lowest validation perplexity (17.87) while maintaining the fastest inference throughput among neural models.
In the second part, an embedding ablation study compares trainable, self-trained (Word2Vec), and pretrained (GloVe) initializations across three neural architectures, revealing that jointly optimized embeddings consistently outperform frozen alternatives.
In the third part, representations from the pre-trained Transformer are transferred to a 7-class intent classification task (SNIPS) via last-token pooling and a single linear layer.
A frozen backbone achieves 91.57\% accuracy (Macro-F1 91.55\%), indicating high linear separability of the learned features; full fine-tuning with differential learning rates raises accuracy to 97.07\% (Macro-F1 97.05\%), with early stopping triggered at epoch~17.
All experiments share a joint vocabulary of 26{,}006 tokens constructed from both the pre-training corpus and the downstream dataset, eliminating out-of-vocabulary issues for domain-specific intent terms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Datasets and Joint Vocabulary Construction}
\label{sec:data}

Two datasets are used. \textbf{WikiText-2} serves as the language modeling corpus, containing approximately 2.1 million tokens from verified Wikipedia articles. Preprocessing removes lines shorter than 10 characters and section headers (lines beginning and ending with~\texttt{=}), followed by uniform lowercasing and whitespace tokenization. For the downstream evaluation, the \textbf{SNIPS} benchmark provides 13{,}784 training utterances distributed across seven intent categories: \texttt{AddToPlaylist}, \texttt{BookRestaurant}, \texttt{GetWeather}, \texttt{PlayMusic}, \texttt{RateBook}, \texttt{SearchCreativeWork}, and \texttt{SearchScreeningEvent}.

A joint vocabulary is constructed through a two-stage process. First, the 20{,}000 most frequent tokens from the WikiText-2 training split are selected. Second, the SNIPS training utterances are tokenized and any previously unseen tokens are appended to the vocabulary. This augmentation adds approximately 6{,}000 domain-specific terms (e.g., \texttt{playlist}, \texttt{restaurant}, \texttt{screening}), yielding a final vocabulary of $|V| = 26{,}006$ tokens, including four reserved special tokens (\texttt{<pad>}, \texttt{<unk>}, \texttt{<bos>}, \texttt{<eos>}). The joint construction eliminates out-of-vocabulary tokens that would otherwise degrade intent classification by collapsing semantically distinct domain terms into a shared \texttt{<unk>} representation.

\subsection{Model Configurations and Training Protocol}
\label{sec:config}

\Cref{tab:hyperparams} consolidates the hyperparameters for all experiments. All neural language models share an embedding dimension of $d = 300$ and are optimized with AdamW ($\text{weight\_decay} = 0.01$). Language model training uses a base learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-4}$ with linear warmup over 1{,}000 steps. For the downstream classifier, a \emph{differential learning rate} strategy is applied: the Transformer backbone receives a learning rate of $5 \times 10^{-5}$, while the classification head is assigned a $10\times$ multiplier ($5 \times 10^{-4}$). This separation prevents large gradient updates from disrupting pre-trained representations while allowing the randomly initialized head to converge quickly. Training is terminated by early stopping with patience of 5~epochs, monitoring validation accuracy. Gradient norms are clipped to 1.0 throughout. All experiments use a fixed random seed of~42.

\section{Part I: Language Model Training and Comparison}
\label{sec:part1}

\subsection{Architectural Overview}
\label{sec:architectures}

Four model families are evaluated, spanning the major paradigms in sequence modeling. The \textbf{Trigram} model provides a non-parametric baseline: it estimates next-token probabilities from bigram context counts with Laplace smoothing ($\alpha = 1.0$), storing co-occurrence statistics as a sparse dictionary to avoid the $O(V^3)$ memory cost of dense tables. Despite its simplicity, it captures strong local patterns and establishes a floor that neural models must exceed to justify their added complexity.

The \textbf{RNN} and \textbf{LSTM} both use 2~stacked layers with 512~hidden units and dropout of~0.5. The critical distinction is the LSTM's gating mechanism: three learned gates (forget, input, output) regulate the flow of information along the temporal axis. The forget gate in particular enables selective erasure of irrelevant context, creating gradient pathways that persist across longer spans than plain RNNs can sustain. Both recurrent models project their hidden states from dimension 512 to 300 via a learned linear layer to align with the shared embedding space for downstream use.

The \textbf{Transformer} follows a decoder-only architecture with 4~layers, 6~attention heads (head dimension~50), and a feed-forward dimension of~1{,}200. Each block applies pre-layer normalization before both the multi-head self-attention and the feed-forward sub-layers, using GELU activation and residual connections. Sinusoidal positional encodings inject position information without adding learnable parameters. An upper-triangular causal mask in the attention computation prevents each position from attending to future tokens, ensuring valid autoregressive generation. Unlike the recurrent models, the Transformer computes all pairwise token dependencies in a single matrix multiplication per layer, eliminating the $O(L)$ sequential bottleneck at the cost of $O(L^2)$ attention complexity.

\label{sec:quant}

Several observations merit discussion. First, the Transformer's parameter count (4.35\,M) is only 10\% larger than the LSTM's (3.94\,M), yet its perplexity is 30\% lower. This gap reflects a fundamental difference in how these architectures utilize their parameters: the Transformer distributes capacity across direct token-to-token attention connections, while recurrent models compress all sequential context into a fixed-width hidden state of dimension~512. This compression creates an information bottleneck---the hidden state must simultaneously encode syntactic structure, semantic content, and positional information into a single vector, inevitably losing fine-grained details that attention mechanisms preserve.

Second, the LSTM outperforms the RNN by a modest margin (25.67 vs.\ 27.49 in PPL), confirming that gating mechanisms provide measurable but limited improvements at the two-layer scale. The relatively narrow gap suggests that on WikiText-2, where most predictive context lies within a few dozen tokens, the RNN's limited effective memory is less of a bottleneck than it would be on tasks requiring longer-range dependencies.

Third, the Trigram achieves a perplexity (25.15) that is competitive with the LSTM, demonstrating that local co-occurrence statistics capture substantial predictive signal in natural language. This result serves as a useful calibration: it shows that a significant portion of next-token prediction can be accomplished by memorizing frequent bigram transitions, and that neural models earn their advantage primarily through their ability to model dependencies beyond the immediate two-token context window.

Regarding inference efficiency, the Transformer processes batches faster than both recurrent models despite having the most parameters. This advantage arises because the Transformer computes all sequence positions in parallel through matrix operations, whereas RNN and LSTM architectures require $L = 64$ sequential hidden-state updates per sample. The result has practical implications: in latency-sensitive applications such as intent routing, the Transformer offers both higher accuracy and lower inference cost.

The convergence curves reveal three noteworthy patterns. First, the Transformer exhibits the steepest initial descent, reaching a perplexity below~40 within 5~epochs, while the RNN and LSTM require approximately 8--10 epochs to reach the same level. This rapid convergence follows from the self-attention mechanism, which provides direct gradient pathways between any two positions in the sequence. In contrast, gradients in recurrent models must traverse up to $L$~time steps before reaching early positions, weakening the learning signal for long-range dependencies.

Second, the LSTM converges to its final perplexity roughly 3--5 epochs ahead of the RNN. This gap is consistent with the theoretical expectation that gating mechanisms accelerate optimization by providing ``shortcut'' gradient pathways through the cell state, which complement the standard hidden-state gradient flow. Both recurrent models exhibit mild perplexity oscillations during mid-training that are largely absent in the Transformer, likely because the sequential gradient flow in recurrent architectures amplifies mini-batch noise.

Third, the Trigram reference line provides practical diagnostic value. Both the LSTM and the Transformer cross below it within the first 10~epochs, confirming that their learned representations capture genuinely longer-range dependencies beyond bigram statistics. The RNN crosses this line only marginally, suggesting that at this scale, its effective context window is only slightly wider than two tokens.

\subsection{Qualitative Evaluation}
\label{sec:qualitative}

To complement the perplexity analysis, we examine text generated by greedy decoding from a shared prompt (\textit{``The historical city of''}). The Trigram produces a repetitive sequence of high-frequency function words (\textit{``a the and in of the \ldots''}), reflecting its inability to maintain coherence beyond the immediate bigram context. The RNN generates grammatically plausible but semantically vacuous text, defaulting to generic phrases (\textit{``the world is a significant and the first \ldots''}) that avoid commitment to any specific topic. The LSTM introduces concrete content---a named entity and a temporal reference (\textit{``London was built in the early 19th century \ldots''})---suggesting that the gating mechanism enables sufficient context retention to maintain topical consistency. The Transformer produces the most coherent output (\textit{``Rome remains a central hub for classical architecture and Mediterranean trade \ldots''}), sustaining both syntactic structure and thematic relevance across the entire generated span.

These qualitative differences align with the perplexity rankings and illustrate a key principle: perplexity measures the average surprisal at each token position, and lower surprisal correlates with the model's ability to assign high probability to contextually appropriate continuations. The Transformer's superior perplexity thus translates directly into more informative and coherent generation, while the Trigram's competitive perplexity score masks its inability to produce globally coherent text---a reminder that aggregate metrics do not fully capture generation quality.

\subsection{Summary of Part~I Findings}
\label{sec:part1_summary}

The results establish a clear architectural hierarchy for language modeling on WikiText-2. The Transformer achieves the best perplexity with the fastest inference, combining representational capacity with computational efficiency. The LSTM provides a solid second option, with gating mechanisms offering consistent advantages over the plain RNN in both convergence speed and final performance. The Trigram, while unable to model long-range structure, remains a useful diagnostic baseline. Based on these results, the Transformer is selected as the backbone for the embedding ablation in Part~II and the downstream transfer experiments in Part~III.
